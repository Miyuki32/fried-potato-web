The PlayStation (1994)

The PlayStation, one of a new generation of 32-bit consoles, signaled Sony's rise to power in the video game world. Also known as the PS One, the PlayStation used compact discs (CDs), heralding the video game industry's move away from cartridges.

PlayStaion2 (2000)

A popular video game console from Sony. DVD based and introduced in the U.S. in 2000 after great success in Japan, the first PS2 models came with a 128-bit, 300 MHz Emotion CPU. Also included was a MIPS R3000 chip for I/O handling and backward compatibility with the earlier PlayStation.

PlayStation3 (2006)

A video game console from Sony that was introduced in late 2006. Containing a high-definition Blu-ray drive that also reads DVDs, CDs and SACD discs, the PS3 uses the Cell processor, which is considerably more powerful than the CPU in the PlayStation 2 (PS2).

PlayStaion4(2013)

The console features a hardware on-the-fly zlib decompression module. The original PS4 model supports up to 1080p and 1080i video standards, while the Pro model supports 4K resolution. The console includes a 500 gigabyte hard drive for additional storage, which can be upgraded by the user.

The PlayStation Potable (2004)

The PlayStation Portable (PSP) was a groundbreaking handheld gaming console released by Sony in 2004. It featured a vibrant 4.3-inch LCD screen, a diverse library of games, and multimedia functionality, including music and video playback. With its sleek design and powerful capabilities, the PSP redefined portable gaming

The PSVITA (2011)

The PS Vita is a handheld gaming console with a 5-inch OLED touchscreen, dual analog sticks, rear touchpad, Wi-Fi/3G connectivity (depending on model), cameras for augmented reality, and a diverse game library.
